Plain-English compliance guide

FTC rules on Google reviews for med spas.

What the FTC actually says about how med spas can ask for, display, and respond to Google reviews. Updated for the 2024 final rule on fake reviews and testimonials. Not legal advice; this is a starting point so you know which questions to bring to your attorney.

Disclaimer. This article is general information, not legal advice. Med spas in particular sit at the intersection of FTC rules, state medical board rules, HIPAA, and Google's own policy. If you are unsure about a specific situation, ask your compliance attorney.

The short version

The FTC's role with reviews is preventing consumer deception. Their position, simplified: reviews must be honest, written by real customers, and the business cannot pay for them or selectively suppress the bad ones.

For a med spa, the practical rules come down to five do-nots and five do's.

Five things the FTC does not allow

  1. Do not pay, discount, or give freebies in exchange for a review. Even a free upgrade or a $5 product credit counts. Unless the incentive is fully disclosed inside the review itself, do not offer it. The cleanest position: never offer one.
  2. Do not let staff, family, or anyone with a "material connection" to the business write reviews. The 2024 final rule made this explicit. Employee reviews are prohibited even if positive and even if true.
  3. Do not write reviews of yourself or your competitors. Both directions are prohibited. Fake competitor takedowns are easy to investigate and the penalty is severe.
  4. Do not ask only happy clients. This is review gating. You must offer the chance to review to a representative sample of patients, not just the ones you expect a 5-star from.
  5. Do not selectively display only positive reviews on your website while hiding negative ones. The 2024 rule treats selective suppression of negative reviews as a deceptive practice.

Five things the FTC explicitly allows

  1. Asking every patient for an honest review. Universal, neutral asks are fine. The script just cannot lean toward "leave us 5 stars."
  2. Following up once on an ask. A single reminder at 7 days is not pressure under the FTC standard.
  3. Replying to reviews publicly. Your replies are not endorsements; they are responses. Reply to negative and positive reviews both.
  4. Removing reviews that violate Google's policy. You can flag fake, off-topic, or spam reviews through Google Business Profile. You cannot remove genuine reviews just because they hurt.
  5. Displaying genuine reviews on your website. If they are real, with consent, and you do not selectively edit them, displaying real reviews is allowed and useful.

The 2024 final rule on fake reviews and testimonials

This is the most important recent change for med spas. The FTC's final rule went into effect in October 2024 and includes:

  • Civil penalties for knowingly buying or selling fake reviews. Penalties can hit $51,744 per violation.
  • Prohibition on reviews from anyone with a material connection to the business (employees, family, paid reviewers).
  • Prohibition on businesses suppressing negative reviews on their own websites while displaying positive ones.
  • Prohibition on misleading "verified" or "100% real" badges that imply review screening when there is none.
  • Liability for AI-generated reviews if attributed to fake humans.

Where med spas trip up the most

Three patterns I see repeatedly in this niche:

  1. The "loyalty program" gray area. "Leave us a review and earn a punch on your loyalty card" is incentivized reviewing. So is "post your review and get a free skincare sample." The penalty: Google can wipe your review history; the FTC can fine.
  2. The "before-after disclaimer" miss. Patient testimonials on your website that imply typical results, without a disclaimer that results vary, can trigger FTC enforcement under the older endorsement guides.
  3. The "we'll review your review" loop. Some spas ask clients to email reviews first so they can "decide" which to publish. That is gating and the FTC has called it out specifically.

How to set up a policy-safe ask

A simple compliant ask script:

  • Ask every patient who completes a paid service.
  • Use a neutral phrase: "We appreciate honest reviews on Google. If you have a minute, here is our link."
  • Do not pre-screen for happy patients only.
  • Do not offer anything in exchange.
  • If they say no, do not ask again until they have come in at least once more.

Anything beyond this and you are wandering into FTC and Google enforcement territory. Worth the conservative position.

Frequently asked questions

Can I offer a discount in exchange for a Google review?

No. Both Google's policy and FTC endorsement guides prohibit incentivized reviews unless the incentive is fully and clearly disclosed in the review itself. For practical purposes at a med spa, do not offer it.

Can I ask only happy patients for reviews?

No. The FTC and Google both call this review gating. You must ask for reviews from a representative sample of clients, not just the ones you expect to leave 5 stars.

Can my staff write reviews?

No. Reviews from employees, family members, or anyone with a material connection to the business are prohibited by the FTC's 2024 final rule on fake reviews.

Can I edit my Google review replies?

Yes, you own your replies. Edit them any time. The FTC rules cover the reviews themselves and your representation of them, not your responses.

Can I display reviews on my website?

Yes if they are genuine and you display them honestly. The FTC's 2024 final rule prohibits selectively displaying only positive reviews while suppressing negative ones.
