Hardest case - results complaints

How to reply when a Google review is about treatment results.

A patient is saying the treatment did not work, the results disappointed them, or they regret the procedure. This is the highest-stakes Google reply a med spa will ever write. Here is the exact approach.

Results complaints are the worst case for med spa public replies because two pressures collide. The legal pressure: HIPAA strictly forbids confirming the patient relationship or naming any clinical detail in public. The owner pressure: every instinct says to explain why the result happened, justify the outcome, or push back on a perceived unfair characterization. Following either instinct without a framework usually makes the review more damaging.

The first rule. Do not engage with the substance of the result complaint in public. Not the procedure. Not the aftercare. Not the timeline. Not the medical record. The public reply has one job: communicate to future patients that your team is calm, professional, and willing to engage privately. Everything else happens off the public review.

The 5-line structure

Every good results-complaint reply follows the same structure. Two sentences minimum, four maximum.

  1. Thanks line. One short line of thanks for the feedback. No qualifiers, no but-clauses.
  2. Acknowledgment of feeling. Reflect the disappointment without confirming what happened. "We are sorry your experience did not meet expectations" is the standard template.
  3. Privacy line. One sentence explaining that you cannot discuss specifics publicly. This protects both you and the reviewer.
  4. Offline path. One sentence inviting them to continue privately. Phone or email; pick one, not both.
  5. Optional closing line. Only if the reply is sitting at 3 lines and feels too clipped. "We would welcome the chance to learn more." Otherwise skip.

Worked examples

1-star review: "The treatment did not work and they refused to refund me"

Thank you for sharing this feedback. We are sorry your experience did not meet expectations. Because patient privacy matters, we cannot discuss specifics publicly. Please reach our team directly at [phone or email] so we can review your concerns.

1-star review: "I am still bruised three weeks later, terrible experience"

Thank you for taking the time to share this. We are sorry to hear your experience did not meet expectations. Patient privacy prevents us from discussing details publicly, but our team would like to follow up directly. Please reach us at [phone or email].

2-star review: "Spent a lot of money and saw no difference"

Thank you for the feedback. We understand the disappointment that comes when expectations are not met, and we are sorry to hear that was your experience. Because privacy matters, we cannot discuss specifics publicly. Please contact us at [phone or email] and our team will follow up.

3-star review: "Results were okay but not what I expected"

Thank you for the honest feedback. We hear that the outcome did not match your expectations, and we appreciate you sharing that. If you would like to discuss further, please reach out at [phone or email].

The 8 things never to say in this kind of reply

  1. Anything that confirms the patient relationship. "When you came in for your treatment" is a HIPAA violation. "Your appointment with us" is a HIPAA violation. Strip all of these.
  2. The treatment name. Naming the procedure publicly is itself a disclosure of protected health information.
  3. Aftercare or follow-up references. "As we discussed in your follow-up" tells the world a patient relationship existed and discusses care.
  4. Results promises or guarantees. "Most patients see results within X weeks" reads as if you are now promising something specific. Avoid.
  5. Blame on the patient. Aftercare non-compliance, missed follow-ups, dietary factors. Even when true, in public this reads as defensive and worsens the reputation impact.
  6. Counterclaims about the review's accuracy. "That is not what happened" is the wrong move. The public reply is not a debate venue.
  7. Refund or re-treatment offers. Any specific offer in public creates legal exposure and invites every future complainant to demand the same.
  8. Comparative references. "Most patients are very happy" or "we have hundreds of 5-star reviews" reads as gaslighting in this context.

What to do offline (the private conversation)

Once the reviewer reaches out through the offline path, you have more room. Even then, follow these:

  • Document everything from the start of the private conversation. Save emails, log calls.
  • Engage with the medical record only with appropriate authorization and counsel review.
  • If remediation is on the table (re-treatment, partial refund, escalation to the medical director), put it in writing with clear scope.
  • Do not require the reviewer to remove the public review in exchange for remediation. That tie is legally questionable and almost always backfires.
  • If the situation is escalating toward legal action, stop replying yourself and engage your malpractice carrier and legal counsel. They will tell you whether to continue the public reply thread or freeze it.

When NOT to reply at all

The review is from an obvious troll account

If the account has no other reviews, was created in the last few days, and the language is wildly off, sometimes the right move is to flag and let it sit unreplied for a few days while Google reviews the flag. If it does not come down, then reply calmly.

The reviewer is threatening legal action

Stop. Engage your malpractice insurer and counsel. Do not reply publicly while a legal claim is being formed. Anything you say will become evidence.

The review contains specific names of staff

Flag the review under Personal Information policy. Wait for Google's response before posting any reply. If Google declines, then reply calmly without naming the staff member back.

Frequently asked questions

Can I explain that the patient did not follow aftercare?

Not publicly. That conversation belongs in the private channel; raising it in public confirms the patient relationship and discloses treatment information.

Can I correct factual errors publicly?

Generally no. The public reply is for future readers, not the reviewer. Public corrections, even accurate ones, sound defensive.

Should I offer re-treatment or a refund publicly?

Never. Move every offer into the private channel.

What if they ask for a before-and-after comparison?

Do not engage publicly. Photo evidence is part of the medical record and cannot be discussed without disclosing PHI.

What if the patient is right that the result is bad?

Same template publicly. Remediation happens in the private channel.

The honest summary

Results complaints are the worst reviews to receive and the most dangerous to reply badly to. The 5-line structure (thanks, acknowledgment, privacy line, offline path, optional closing) handles 95 percent of cases. The other 5 percent need counsel before you post a public reply. Resist every instinct to explain, justify, or counter in public. The future patient reading the review is not weighing the substance; they are weighing how you handle adversity.
