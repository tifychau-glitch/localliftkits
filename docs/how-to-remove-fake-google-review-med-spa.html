Reputation - fake review removal

How to remove a fake Google review for a med spa.

What Google actually removes, what it will never touch, and the exact flagging flow that works. Plus what to do when removal fails, because most of the time it does.

Most med spa owners hit this moment eventually. A 1-star review shows up from a name nobody on the team recognizes, or it is so over the top it cannot be real, or it is obviously a competitor. The instinct is to fight it. Sometimes that works. Most of the time it does not, and the energy is better spent elsewhere. This guide is honest about both.

The first thing to know: Google removes reviews that violate its policies. It does not remove reviews that are just negative, unfair, or factually wrong in your opinion. The success of any removal effort depends entirely on whether the review hits a policy violation, not on whether you believe it is unfair.

What Google actually removes

From the public Google policies page, the categories that lead to removal are specific. The review has to clearly hit one of these:

Policy violationWhat it means in plain English
Fake contentThe reviewer was never a customer. No actual visit, no actual interaction.
Conflict of interestReviewer is a competitor, ex-employee, family of competitor, or anyone with a financial stake against the business.
Off-topicReview is about something unrelated to the business (political rant, personal feud, the parking lot of the strip mall, etc.).
Harassment or hate speechPersonal attacks, slurs, threats. The bar is real harassment, not "they were rude about us."
Personal informationReview reveals personal info about staff or other clients (name, phone, address, medical info).
SpamRepetitive posting, posting from many accounts, irrelevant promotional content.
Illegal, terrorist, sexually explicit contentSelf-explanatory. Rare for med spa reviews.

What Google will never remove

  • A real customer who had a real bad experience and described it.
  • A review that is unfair but factually mostly accurate.
  • A review that misunderstood what happened but is still opinion.
  • A 1-star with no text at all from a real customer.
  • A review you simply disagree with.

Reading the review honestly is step one. If the reviewer was a real client and just did not like their visit, removal will not work. The energy goes into the public reply, not the flag.

Step-by-step: how to flag a review

  1. Sign in to your Google Business Profile. Search your business name on Google while logged into the email that owns the listing.
  2. Find the review. Either through the Reviews tab in the Business Profile dashboard, or on the live Google Maps listing.
  3. Click the three-dot menu on the review. On the listing view it appears as three vertical dots next to the review. Select "Flag as inappropriate" or "Report review".
  4. Pick the violation category. Be specific. Use Conflict of Interest if you have any evidence the reviewer is a competitor. Use Fake Content if there is no record of the customer in your system. Use Off-Topic if the review is about something unrelated. Pick the strongest fit, not the most dramatic option.
  5. Submit and wait. Typical response window is 3 to 14 days. Google does not always notify you of the decision.
Optional second step: if the review is clearly violating policy and the first flag is ignored, file a second time through the Google Business Profile support contact form. Cite the specific policy and attach any evidence (screenshots, dates, account links). Be calm and factual in the form. Aggressive escalation does not help.

What to do while you wait for Google

Reviews stay public during the flagging review. The right move is to also reply publicly, calmly, and HIPAA-safely, even while the flag is pending. That way readers who land on the page during the wait see a measured response, not silence.

The reply should:

  • Not confirm the reviewer was a patient. (HIPAA.)
  • Not name a treatment, condition, or visit detail. (HIPAA.)
  • Not call the review fake or accuse the reviewer of lying in public.
  • Offer one offline contact path.
  • Stay 2 to 4 sentences.

A flat, brief, professional reply often does more for the reader than a successful removal would have, because most prospects scrolling reviews are not counting 1-stars. They are reading the owner's responses.

When removal fails: the long-game playbook

Most flagged reviews do not come down. Even clear policy violations sometimes survive review. The realistic plan is to bury the review with new positive reviews from real happy clients. The math is simple: a 4.8 average across 60 reviews barely moves when one 1-star is added. A 4.6 across 12 reviews tanks. Volume is the moat.

If you are below 25 reviews, the priority is asking widely and gently every week. The 6-script guide on the resources page covers how. The Growth Kit packages the tracker and weekly workflow for this.

When NOT to try to remove

The review is real but harsh

Reply publicly, reach out privately, learn what to fix. Do not flag. Flagging real reviews can get your listing suspended.

You disagree with their account

Opinion is protected. Reply calmly, do not contradict, offer offline path.

It is anonymous and you cannot tell

If there is no clear policy violation, flagging will not work. Reply and move on.

It is a long-time client having a bad day

Pick up the phone. Most long-term clients will edit or remove the review themselves once the underlying issue is resolved. That is the highest-success removal path that exists.

Frequently asked questions

Can Google really remove a fake review?

Yes, when the review clearly violates a policy. Reviews that are just negative will not be removed.

How long does Google take to remove a flagged review?

3 to 14 days is typical. Clear-cut cases come down in 48 hours. Borderline cases can sit for weeks or never resolve.

What if the reviewer is a competitor?

That is a Conflict of Interest policy violation. Flag under that category and include evidence if you have any.

Can I sue the reviewer?

Almost never the right tool. Cost, time, and the Streisand effect of a lawsuit usually make it counterproductive.

What if the review will not come down?

Reply calmly and HIPAA-safely, then bury it with steady new positive reviews from real happy clients.

The honest bottom line

Most reviews you want gone will not come down. Spending 6 hours fighting a 1-star is 6 hours not spent generating 8 new 5-stars. The smart math is: flag obvious policy violations once, reply publicly and calmly, and then put the energy into your review-request workflow.
