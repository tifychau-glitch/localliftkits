Strategy - review math

How many Google reviews does a med spa need to rank?

Plain-English math on review count, rating, and velocity for med spa local SEO. Realistic numbers, no hype, with the trade-off most guides skip.

If you are searching this question, you are usually one of two owners. Either you are 6 months in, watching competitors with 80 plus reviews sitting in the Map Pack while your 12 reviews sit on page two, and wondering what the actual number is. Or you are about to open and trying to plan how aggressive your review-ask process needs to be. Either way, this article is the realistic answer, not the marketing-blog one.

The short version: Google has confirmed publicly that "Review count and score are factored into local search ranking. More reviews and positive ratings can improve your business's local ranking." That is the official line. The unofficial, market-by-market reality is below.

The honest benchmarks (med spa specific)

These are pulled from open-source local SEO surveys and ranking studies that focus on local service businesses. They are floors, not magic numbers, and your specific market may be denser or thinner.

Practical move: open Google Maps, search "med spa near me" from your business address, look at the top 3 listings, and read their review counts. Your floor is roughly the count of the third-place listing. Beating that is the realistic short-term goal.

What Google actually rewards (in order)

  1. Rating. Below 4.0 is a hard ceiling. You will not rank well in a competitive med spa market with a 3.8 average no matter how many reviews you have.
  2. Volume. Once you are above 4.5, more reviews compound. Each one is a tiny trust signal and a chance to widen the gap.
  3. Velocity. Google looks at how recent the reviews are. A med spa with 80 reviews where the most recent is 8 months old looks dormant. A med spa with 25 reviews where the most recent is 4 days ago looks alive.
  4. Diversity of reviewers. 30 reviews from 30 different people who post other reviews on other businesses is a stronger signal than 30 from accounts that only ever reviewed you.
  5. Reply density. Owner replies on a high percentage of reviews are a small but real signal of active management.

The trade-off most guides skip

Asking aggressively to chase a number tends to break two things. First, it pushes staff to ask only happy patients (review gating), which violates Google policy and the FTC's 2024 final rule. Second, it can trigger Google's spam filters when reviews appear in clusters from similar IP addresses, similar phrasing, or similar account profiles. We have seen med spas get whole batches wiped because they pushed a one-day campaign too hard.

The boring, sustainable approach (steady, ask widely, opt-out, follow up once at 7 days) almost always outperforms the burst approach over 6 months. Math example: 4 new reviews a week, every week, is 200 a year. That moves a med spa from "near the bottom of page 1" to "fighting for the Map Pack" in 12 months in most markets.

The 90 / 180 / 365 day plan

Days 0 to 90

Get to 25 reviews. Audit your last 90 days of patient visits and run a one-time outreach to clients you have not yet asked. From day 30 onward, switch to the steady-state model: ask every happy patient, send link by text, one 7-day follow-up. The 6-script guide on the resources hub covers the language.

Days 90 to 180

Get to 50 reviews. By now the ask should be automatic at checkout. Audit reply density: every review should have a calm owner reply within 72 hours. Use the free reply generator on this site or the swipe file templates.

Days 180 to 365

Compound. 3 to 6 new reviews per month, steady. Reply to every one. Watch for review velocity drops longer than 30 days and re-energize the staff prompt. The volume calculator on this site projects exactly where you will be in 12 months at a given weekly rate.

When the number does not matter

  • You are below 4.2 average. Fix the experience first, then chase volume. More volume on a low rating just locks the rating in.
  • You operate primarily by referral or off-platform. Chase reviews only enough to look legitimate, then put the energy elsewhere.
  • You have a non-rev-generating use case (a holding listing, a service-area business without a storefront). Different game.

Frequently asked questions

Is review count a real Google ranking factor?

Yes. Google explicitly lists review count and score among the prominence signals for local search.

How many reviews do I need to rank top 3?

No exact threshold. Floor is usually 30 to 50 in most med spa markets, 80 plus in dense urban markets, with a 4.5 plus average.

Does velocity matter more than total count?

Both matter. For new or small med spas, velocity (recent reviews per month) is the bigger short-term lever.

Will one 1-star drop my rank?

Against 30 plus other reviews, minor impact. Against 5 to 10 reviews, it can drop the average enough to lose a position. The defense is volume.

Count or rating first?

Count first if you are below 25 reviews. Rating first if you are above 25 and below 4.5.

The bottom line

The realistic answer to "how many reviews to rank" for most med spas is: 30 to 60 with a 4.5 plus average, gathered steadily over 6 to 12 months, with consistent owner replies. The strategic move is not to hit a number. It is to build the workflow that produces 3 to 6 new reviews a month forever.
