Review requests - asking without pressure

How to ask med spa patients for Google reviews without being pushy.

Most med spa owners feel weird asking for Google reviews. They worry it will sound transactional, that the patient will say yes politely and then never do it, or worse, that asking will somehow make the relationship feel commercial.

The truth is the opposite. Patients who love their experience usually want to say so. They just need a small, easy moment to do it. The job is not to convince them. The job is to make it effortless and give them permission to opt out.

Quick note on policy: never offer a discount, perk, or freebie in exchange for a review. Google's policy bans incentivized reviews, and the penalty is severe. Ask for honest feedback only. Every script below follows this rule.

The 6 step framework

  1. Ask at the right moment. Right after a visible win. The result they love, the thank-you they say first, the moment they look in the mirror.
  2. Get permission, then send the link. Permission framing removes pressure. After they say yes, send the link by text so they can act before they leave the parking lot.
  3. Keep it short. Long explanations feel salesy. Patients trust short asks.
  4. Give them an opt-out. Phrases like "no pressure if it is not your thing" or "only if you have a minute" build trust and actually increase the response rate.
  5. Follow up once. Seven days. One gentle nudge. Never two. Never three.
  6. Thank publicly when it lands. A calm public reply tells the next patient your team pays attention.

Script 1: in-person ask at checkout (first-time client)

For a first-time client who loved their visit. This works face-to-face at the front desk after their appointment.

If you have a minute later, would you be open to leaving us a quick Google review? It really helps other people find us. I will text you the link so you can do it from your couch. No pressure if it is not your thing.

Script 2: text after they leave (sent the same day)

Send within 2 hours of their appointment. Brief, casual, link at the end.

Hi [First Name], thank you again for coming in today. If you have a sec, our Google review page is here: [link]. Anything honest is appreciated. If now is not the right time, no problem at all.

Script 3: email after a repeat visit

For a repeat client who has been with you a while. Slightly warmer, references the relationship.

Hi [First Name], Hope you are doing well after [Tuesday]. Quick favor if you have a minute: would you be open to leaving us a Google review? You have been with us long enough to have a real sense of the team, so your words would mean more than a brand new client's. Here is the link if you want to: [link] Either way, thank you for trusting us with your care.

Script 4: the 7 day follow-up (if they said yes but did not post)

One follow-up, gentle, easy to ignore. If they do not respond, leave it alone.

Hi [First Name], no pressure at all on this one. I know life gets busy. Here is the Google review link again if you ever want to drop one: [link]. If not, no worries.

Script 5: in-person ask when the patient says thank you first

The strongest moment. Use the energy.

Thank you for saying that. If you ever feel like sharing it publicly, a Google review would really help us. I can send you the link. Only if you want to.

Script 6: when a patient says they will leave a review but you doubt it

Polite, low-key, makes follow-through more likely without nagging.

Awesome, thank you. I will send the link right now to make it easy. Even one sentence helps a lot. And if you forget, no worries at all.

What not to do

  • Do not offer a discount, free service, or perk in exchange for a review. Google penalizes incentivized reviews and your listing can be wiped.
  • Do not ask only the patients you think will say yes. Ask widely and give an honest opt-out.
  • Do not send a long explanation about why reviews matter. Short asks convert better.
  • Do not follow up more than once. Two nudges feels pushy. Three burns trust.
  • Do not ask for a "5 star review." Ask for "an honest review." Asking for a specific rating violates Google's policy.
  • Do not stop asking after one round of yeses. Asking every happy client every visit is normal and builds a steady stream.

Frequently asked questions

Is it okay to ask every patient for a review?

Yes, as long as you ask after a positive experience and give an opt-out. Asking universally and gently is far better than only asking the ones you think will say yes.

Can I offer a discount in exchange for a review?

No. Google's policy bans incentivized reviews. You can lose the listing or get all your reviews wiped. Always ask for honest feedback only.

Should I ask in person or by text?

Both. Ask in person first to get permission. Then send the link by text so they can do it on their phone before they leave the parking lot.

How long should I wait to follow up?

Seven days is standard. One follow-up only. Two follow-ups starts to feel pushy and erodes trust.

What if the client says they had a great visit but never leaves a review?

Normal. Most happy clients never write a review unless prompted. Your job is to make it easy to say yes and easy to say no. Both build trust.
