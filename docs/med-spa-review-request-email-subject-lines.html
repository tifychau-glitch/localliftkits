Review requests - email subject lines

Med spa review request email subject lines that get opened.

30 plain-English subject lines you can use right now, organized by stage. HIPAA-safe, never incentivized, written for the phone preview pane where most patients will actually read them.

The subject line decides whether your review request gets read. On a phone, most patients see 40 to 50 characters of subject and the first 3 to 5 words of preview text. That tiny window is the whole game.

The math: at a typical med spa, a review request email gets opened by 50 to 70 percent of recipients when the subject line is well written, and 20 to 35 percent when it is generic or salesy. On 100 requests a month that is the difference between roughly 6 new reviews and roughly 2. Over a year, it is the difference between a Google profile that climbs and one that stalls.

Five rules every subject line below follows: (1) Never ask for "5 stars" or any specific rating, which violates Google policy. (2) Never offer or imply a discount or freebie. (3) Never name a treatment, condition, or anything that could be read as protected health information. (4) Keep it under 50 characters. (5) Lead with the most important word; the rest may be truncated on a phone.

After the first appointment (within 48 hours)

For a client who just had their first visit. Tone: warm, low-pressure, short.

Thanks for visiting us today Quick favor from [Med Spa Name] [First name], a small thank-you ask One small thing if you have a sec A quiet thank-you, no rush

After a repeat or recurring visit

For a client who has been with you a while. Slightly warmer, references continuity.

Always good to see you, [First name] A quick favor after today [First name], a thank-you ask from the team Glad to see you back this week One small ask, only if it is easy

The 7-day follow-up

Use only if they said yes in person but did not post. One follow-up only. Two starts to feel pushy.

No rush, just sharing the link again Here is that Google link if you want it A gentle nudge, ignore if life is busy The link from our chat last week Only if it is easy, no worries either way

After a strong moment in the room

When the patient said something kind first, or you got a clear in-room thank-you. Use that energy, but stay general.

Thank you for what you said today That made our day, [First name] A small follow-up from earlier A quiet thank-you for what you shared We appreciated your words today

Casual and short (when in doubt)

Short subjects often win on the phone. These are safe defaults that work across most stages.

A quick favor One small ask Thank you, [First name] A small note from [Med Spa Name] [First name], a quiet ask

Reply-style subjects (for ongoing email threads)

If you are replying to an existing thread (appointment confirmation, post-visit message), keeping the existing subject and replying tends to outperform starting a fresh thread.

Re: your visit today Re: your appointment on [date] Re: hi from [Med Spa Name] Re: thanks for coming in Re: see you soon

Five subject lines to never send

Each of these breaks at least one of the rules above. Use them as a checklist of what to delete from your drafts.

  • "Leave us a 5 star review!" Asking for a specific rating violates Google's policy and can get reviews wiped or the listing flagged.
  • "Free product for your honest review" Incentivized reviews are prohibited even when honesty is requested. The FTC's 2024 rule is explicit on this.
  • "How was your Botox today?" Naming the treatment in the subject line is a HIPAA risk because the subject may be visible in notification previews to anyone who picks up the phone.
  • "URGENT: Review request" Urgency framing on a non-urgent ask burns trust the moment they open it.
  • "Please please please leave a review" Begging tone signals weakness and lowers open and conversion rate. Ask once, calmly, give an opt-out.

How to A/B test these in practice

You do not need a marketing tool. Pick two subject lines from the lists above, send each to 25 patients over the same week, and compare open rates by checking who responded or clicked. Keep the winner, retire the loser, draft a new challenger. Over 60 days you will have a subject line tuned to your specific client base.

One caveat: open rate is not the only metric. A subject line that opens at 70 percent but converts to a review at 5 percent is worse than one that opens at 50 percent and converts at 12 percent. Track both.

Frequently asked questions

What is a good open rate for a med spa review request email?

For one-to-one emails sent within 48 hours of a visit, 50 to 70 percent is healthy. Below 40 percent usually means the subject line is too generic or too salesy, or the email is being sent too long after the visit.

Should I personalize the subject line with the patient's name?

Yes, when the email is going to one person. First-name personalization in the subject lifts opens. Avoid the treatment name in the subject line for HIPAA-preview reasons.

Can I put "leave a 5 star review" in the subject line?

No. Asking for a specific star rating violates Google's review policy and can get the listing flagged.

How long should a review request subject line be?

4 to 8 words. On a phone, only the first 40 to 50 characters reliably show. Lead with the most important word.

Should the subject line mention Google?

Optional. Saying "Google review" is honest and clear. A softer subject like "quick favor" often opens better. Either works if the body is short and clear.
