Filter triggers - plain English

Why Google reviews disappear from a med spa listing.

The 7 most common reasons real reviews quietly vanish, how to get them reinstated, and the asking patterns that keep new reviews from getting filtered in the first place.

You log in to your Google Business Profile and notice the review count dropped. Or a regular client texts to say their review is missing. Or the average ticked up but the count is lower than yesterday. Reviews disappearing from a med spa listing is a normal experience and usually not what owners assume. Here is the plain-English version.

The first thing to know: Google does not tell you when it removes a review, and it does not tell the reviewer. The filter runs continuously in the background. Most disappearances are automated, not human decisions, and most are based on the reviewer's account signals rather than your business's behavior.

The 7 most common filter triggers

TriggerWhat it looks like
New reviewer accountThe reviewer made the Google account in the last few days and you are their first or second review. Common signal of incentivized review.
Single-business reviewerThe account has only ever reviewed your business. Google reads this as a possible fake or family-member review.
Same-network postingThe review was posted from the same IP address as the business (e.g., the patient posted while on your guest Wi-Fi). Treated as self-review risk.
Burst of reviews5 or more reviews in 24 to 48 hours after a long quiet stretch. Filter assumes a campaign rather than organic.
Reviewer flagged elsewhereThe reviewer's account got marked for spam on a different business. Google can sweep their other reviews retroactively.
Policy-violating contentReview contains a phone number, a competitor name, a slur, an off-topic rant. Auto-filtered by content rules.
Manual flag from someoneA competitor, an angry ex-employee, or any user can flag a review. If Google's review of the flag finds a policy issue, it comes down.

What this means for you

Most disappearances do not signal that you did something wrong. The single biggest contributor in med spa-land is the reviewer-account signals: a new patient leaves a review on their first-ever Google account, and the spam filter strips it. The patient is real. The visit was real. The review was real. The account looks suspect to a machine.

That is also why frantically asking the reviewer to repost almost never works. The filter is reading their account, not their words. A second post from the same account will likely get filtered the same way.

What to do when a review disappears

  1. Check whether it actually disappeared. Sort the listing by "newest" and "lowest rated" and scan for the review. Sometimes a review is just sorted lower than expected, not removed.
  2. Document. If you can, screenshot the live review before it goes (best done as a habit, not after the fact). Record the reviewer's name, date, and rating.
  3. Submit a reinstatement request. In Google Business Profile, go to Support and choose "Reviews" then "Removed by Google." Provide the reviewer's name and approximate date. Explain why the review was legitimate. Do not be aggressive; success rate is modest.
  4. Keep asking new clients. Reinstatement is slow and unreliable. Replacing the lost trust signal with new reviews from diverse, established reviewer accounts is faster.
  5. Do not retaliate. Reaching out to the reviewer to complain or asking them to repost is rarely productive. Move on.

How to ask in a way that doesn't trigger the filter

The asking pattern matters more than most owners realize. The boring sustainable approach almost always survives the filter; the burst approach almost always trips it. Specifics:

  • Ask from a personal device, not in-house. Patients posting reviews on the spa's guest Wi-Fi is a known filter trigger. Suggest they leave from home or on their phone's cellular data.
  • Spread requests out. 3 to 6 per week beats 30 in a single Saturday. The filter looks for unnatural velocity.
  • Ask widely, not selectively. Review gating (only asking happy clients) leads to all-5-star spikes, which the filter reads as suspicious. Ask everyone and let the natural mix happen.
  • Never offer incentives. Even a small "thanks for the review, here's $5 off your next visit" can lead to a retroactive wipe of every review you have.
  • Verify reviewer accounts are real. A brand-new account posting one review and disappearing is filter bait. Encourage patients to use their existing Google account if they have one.

When reviews disappear AND you know what happened

You ran a too-aggressive campaign

Stop the campaign immediately. Switch to the boring sustainable approach. Reinstatement is unlikely; the filter usually got it right.

You incentivized reviews

This is the worst case. Google has wiped entire businesses' review history retroactively for this. Stop, audit your last 6 months of communications for incentive language, and consider a clean restart with policy-safe asking.

A competitor flagged you

Submit reinstatement requests for any legitimate reviews that came down. Document the pattern. Repeated bad-faith flagging is itself a policy violation and Google can act on it eventually.

A specific patient's review just vanished

Probably the patient's account signals, not yours. Submit the reinstatement request, ask the patient if they want to chase it, but do not pressure. Move on to asking new clients.

Frequently asked questions

Does Google really remove reviews automatically?

Yes. Google runs automated spam and policy filters continuously and accepts manual flags. Reviews can vanish without notification to either side.

Why did my real 5-star review disappear?

Usually the reviewer's account signals (new account, single-business reviewer, posting from your network). Google does not announce the specific reason.

Can I get a filtered review reinstated?

Sometimes. Use the Google Business Profile help form, include the reviewer name and date, and explain why it was legitimate. Modest success rate, not zero.

Will incentivized reviews get wiped?

Yes, often retroactively. Severe cases lead to listing suspension.

Should I ask the reviewer to repost?

Generally no. If their account triggered the filter, the second post will likely be filtered too.

The honest summary

Most disappearing reviews are downstream of two things: the reviewer's account looks suspicious to a machine, or the asking pattern was too clustered. Both are fixable. Ship a steady weekly request workflow, ask widely with a real opt-out, and accept a small attrition rate as the cost of being on the platform.
