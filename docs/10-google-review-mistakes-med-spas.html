10 Google review mistakes med spas make.

Pulled from real Google profiles, real owner forums, and 6 months of reading bad replies in the wild. Each one has a specific fix and a one-line example. Ordered by how much damage they do.

1 Replying within the first hour

The defensive reply happens here. Owner sees the notification at 9:47am, blood pressure spikes, and the reply lands at 9:52am. That five-minute reply is what future patients screenshot.

Fix: wait at least 24 business hours. Have a teammate read your draft before you post. Schedule "no replies before tomorrow" as a team rule.

2 Confirming the treatment by name

The single most common HIPAA exposure. The reviewer mentions Botox in their complaint, so the owner writes "We're sorry your Botox experience..." That sentence just confirmed the reviewer was a patient AND what they had.

Fix: never name the treatment. Use "experience" or "visit" or skip the noun entirely. Test your draft in the free HIPAA risk auditor before posting.
Bad: "We're sorry your dermaplaning session did not meet expectations."
Better: "We're sorry your experience did not meet expectations."

3 Offering a discount or comp publicly

The owner means well: "Please come back, the next service is on us." The next unhappy patient screenshots that and asks for the same. Now the reply is your pricing policy.

Fix: never offer anything in the public reply. Move offers to a private channel. "Please contact us at [phone or email] so we can make this right" is the public version.

4 Asking only happy patients (review gating)

It feels efficient: send the review link only to clients you know loved their visit. Google and the FTC both call this review gating. It can wipe your review history and trigger a penalty.

Fix: ask every paid client. The "ask widely, give a real opt-out" model is the only compliant pattern. See the full breakdown in the FTC rules article.

5 Arguing point by point with the reviewer

Owner sees a factually wrong claim ("they were 45 minutes late") and rebuts it ("we have you signed in at 9:02"). Even when accurate, the public reads it as defensive. The reviewer escalates. The thread becomes a fight.

Fix: acknowledge the feeling, not the fact. "We are sorry your experience did not meet expectations" lets you stay calm without conceding anything.

6 Quoting from the patient chart

"Per your chart, we did discuss potential bruising in your consultation." That sentence is one HIPAA exposure stacked on another: confirms patient status, confirms a chart exists, quotes from it.

Fix: never quote or paraphrase chart contents in public. Even denying a chart claim publicly is a violation. Move that conversation to a private channel and document internally.

7 Letting staff write reviews

Owner thinks it is harmless: "Lisa loves working here, she should leave us a 5-star." The FTC's 2024 final rule explicitly prohibits reviews from anyone with a material connection to the business. Penalty: up to $51,744 per violation.

Fix: staff cannot write reviews. Family cannot write reviews. If a staffer wants to advocate, ask them to refer real friends to leave honest reviews instead.

8 No follow-up on review requests

The front desk asks at checkout, the client says yes, then forgets. The ask converts at maybe 1 in 4. Without a follow-up, the other 3 are lost.

Fix: one follow-up at day 7. One. Not day 1 (too pushy), not day 14 (too late). See the request scripts guide for the exact one-line follow-up that works.

9 Generic copy-paste replies to every review

Owner writes one "Thank you for your kind review" reply and uses it for everything. Future patients read 12 identical replies in a row and infer the business does not actually read what people write.

Fix: use templates as starting points, then customize one sentence per reply. The lazy version is "thank you, [Specific phrase or word from their review]." That two-second edit changes the perception entirely.

10 Ignoring reviews older than a week

Owner replies to fresh reviews but the 3-month-old ones sit unanswered. Future patients scroll back, see a chunk of silence, infer the business does not care after the moment passes.

Fix: once per month, reply to every unanswered review including old ones. Same templates work. The reviewer probably will not even notice; the future patient scrolling your profile definitely will.
