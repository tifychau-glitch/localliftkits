Pre-crisis - threatened review

When a patient threatens to leave a bad Google review.

A calm pre-crisis playbook for med spas. What to say, what to never say, and how to keep the situation legal and HIPAA-safe before anything gets posted.

Almost every owner gets one of these eventually. A patient is unhappy. They are angry on the phone, or they sent an email with the words "I will be leaving a review" in it, or they mentioned it at checkout. The clock starts. You have a small window to handle it well and a smaller one to handle it badly.

The first thing to know: the threat is usually a request in disguise. Most patients who say "I will leave a review" want resolution. The review is leverage. Treat the underlying request seriously and the leverage often goes away on its own.

Three things to never do, even once: (1) Do not offer a refund or perk explicitly conditional on them not posting a review. That tie creates legal and Google-policy risk and almost always backfires. (2) Do not ask them to sign an NDA or non-disparagement clause; the federal Consumer Review Fairness Act of 2016 makes most of these unenforceable and exposes the business to FTC action. (3) Do not threaten back. No mention of lawsuits, defamation, or removing the review. It will end up screenshotted in the review.

The 4-step pre-crisis playbook

  1. Listen without resolving. The first call or message is not the place to fix it. Let them say everything. Take notes. Confirm you understand what they want.
  2. Buy time. "I want to make this right and I want to get the details correct. Can I get back to you within 24 hours with a specific answer?" A pause de-escalates almost every threatened-review situation.
  3. Make a real offer on the merits. Refund, redo, partial credit, escalation to the owner, whatever is appropriate. Offer it because it is right, not to suppress the review. Never tie it to the review verbally or in writing.
  4. Confirm in writing. Email after the call summarizing what was agreed. Plain language, no legalese, no review mention.

What to say on the phone (script)

For the first call, when emotions are high.

I hear you. I want to make sure I have the full picture before I respond, so I am going to listen and take notes. After that I will come back with what we can do. Is that okay?

If they bring up the review specifically:

I understand. My focus right now is making this right with you, not the review. We will take care of you regardless of what you decide to post.

That second line is the load-bearing one. It refuses the implicit trade and reframes the situation as your team taking the high road. It also reads well later, even if the conversation gets quoted in the review.

What to put in writing (email template)

After the call, send a short confirmation.

Hi [First name], Thank you for the call earlier. I want to confirm what we discussed and what we are offering. What I heard: [summary in their words, not yours] What we are offering: [specific resolution, timing, who to contact] If anything in this is off, please reply and I will fix it. We will follow through either way. [Owner name] [Med Spa Name]
Two things this email does on purpose. It does not mention the review. It uses their words for the complaint, which makes it harder for them to later claim you misunderstood.

When the review still gets posted

Sometimes the patient posts anyway. That is okay. A calm, HIPAA-safe public reply often weighs more in a reader's mind than the original complaint.

  • Do not confirm the patient relationship. (HIPAA.)
  • Do not name the treatment. (HIPAA.)
  • Do not contradict their facts in public, even if they are wrong.
  • Offer one offline path. ("Please reach our owner directly at...")
  • Keep it 2 to 4 sentences. Long replies look defensive.

Edge cases

The patient is being abusive on the phone

You are allowed to end the call. Document the time, who took the call, and what was said. Continue the conversation in writing so there is a clear record. If language escalates to specific threats of harm, that is a police matter, not a customer-service matter.

The patient demands a refund explicitly to not post

Decouple the two in your reply. "I can offer X based on what you described. That offer stands regardless of whether you choose to post a review." That sentence is the right answer almost every time.

The complaint is genuinely unjustified

Still treat the underlying experience as real to them. You do not have to admit fault. "I am sorry the visit landed this way. Here is what we can do given the situation: ..." is enough.

It is a competitor, not a real patient

You will not know for sure until the review is posted. Do not accuse them on the call. If the review lands and is clearly false or violates Google's policy (fake account, slander, off-topic), report it through Google's flagging flow. Most clearly-fake reviews come down within 7 to 21 days.

Frequently asked questions

Is it legal for a patient to threaten a bad review unless I refund them?

In extreme cases this may meet your state's definition of extortion, but most situations are a normal complaint dressed in pressure language. Treat it as a complaint to resolve on the merits, not a legal matter, unless the language escalates.

Can I make the patient sign an NDA or non-disparagement clause?

No. The federal Consumer Review Fairness Act of 2016 makes most non-disparagement clauses in form contracts unenforceable and exposes the business to FTC enforcement.

Should I offer a refund to prevent the review?

Offer a refund when it is the right resolution on the merits. Do not tie it to the review. Saying "we will refund if you do not post" invites a worse review later and creates legal risk.

What if the patient is being abusive on the phone?

Document the call, then end it calmly. Continue in writing. You are allowed to refuse to engage with abusive contact.

If the review still gets posted, what do I do?

Reply once, calmly, without confirming the patient relationship or any treatment details, and offer to continue offline. Reasonable readers weight the calm reply heavily.

The deeper point

Most threatened reviews are a stress test of your customer-service process, not your reputation strategy. If your process treats every threat as a real grievance to resolve on the merits, the threat usually evaporates and you get a calmer patient instead of a worse review. If your process treats the threat as a transaction to suppress, you end up with both a refund spent and a review posted anyway.
